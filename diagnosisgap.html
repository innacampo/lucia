Underlying Factors & Misdiagnoses

The Long Road to an ADHD Diagnosis

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) often presents differently in girls and women, leading to significant delays. While boys are often diagnosed in childhood due to hyperactive symptoms, women are frequently diagnosed in their 30s or 40s after years of struggling with inattentive symptoms.

Common Misdiagnoses for Women

Symptoms of complex conditions like Autism and Bipolar Disorder in women are frequently attributed to more "common" diagnoses, delaying appropriate treatment.

Factors Contributing to Diagnostic Gaps

The path to misdiagnosis is paved with systemic biases and a historical lack of research focused on women's health. Key factors include:

  • Gender-Biased Criteria: Diagnostic criteria for many conditions were based on studies conducted primarily on men, failing to capture female symptom presentation.
  • Symptom Internalization: Women are more likely to internalize symptoms (e.g., inattentiveness, anxiety), which are less disruptive and noticeable than externalized symptoms (e.g., hyperactivity).
  • Societal Expectations: Women are often socialized to be compliant and to mask their difficulties, a phenomenon known as "camouflaging," which can hide underlying distress.
  • Dismissal by Clinicians: Clinicians may attribute a woman's symptoms to stress or hormones rather than exploring an underlying disorder.
